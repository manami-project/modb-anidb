One day, an otaku high school student Aki Tomoya has a fateful encounter with a girl amidst the beautiful cherry blossom trees. This meeting inspires Tomoya to design his very own gal-game (dating-simulation video game) featuring a heroine modelled after the girl he saw. In order to make his desire a reality, Tomoya must persuade a few eccentric creators such as the ace member of the art club, Sawamura Spencer Eriri and the school's top student, Kasumigaoka Utaha to join his development team. Meanwhile, Tomoya is shocked to learn that the girl he idolized as his muse for this whole project was none other than his boring classmate, Katou Megumi! But, this does not dissuade Tomoya. He is determined to give Megumi a total make-over within his game and raise her to be the absolute girlfriend that every man desires...!

by irohma on 2015-07-13 19:05

Review
Saenai Heroine no Sodatekata (Saekano) was translated to the western market as Saekano: How to Raise a Boring Girlfriend, which is an interesting name, but I find it more fitting to call it Saekano: How to Raise a Boring Protagonist. Why that? Well, let's elaborate.

Animated by the guys behind the popular and outstanding A1-Pictures, Saekano is a parody mixed with teen romance and your typical harem elements about an otaku who dreams of crafting his very own dating sim. It starts depicting otakus as alien beings rejected by society and perverted by an industry that feeds on sexuality, loli heroines, and virtual worlds. As it goes on, however, it slowly shows that those people are not that abnormal, they are just... people.
  1. The concept is there...
    You are instantly thrown into the main objective of the plot as Saekano starts. Aki Tomoya has an ordinary experience of meeting an extraordinary girl and from there he decides he must make a dating sim about her, even though he barely remembers anything about the girl herself. From there Saekano tries to craft an otaku protagonist who claims to prefer 2D girls over 3D girls (silly terms, annoying dramatization of otakus indeed) and puts him to work to achieve his dream. However...

    He is already a boring harem protagonist from the start
    Yeah. He has his childhood friend, he is a creepy guy who somehow not only befriended the two most popular and cute girls from school, but has both of them in love with him. Ah, and those cute popular girls are also hardcore otakus in their daily lives: one draws adult material and the other is a famous young novelist. Add to that the fact Aki meets a dumb girl he wants to turn into a gal-ge heroine and she instantly becomes his minion and also falls in love with him and well... you already got a big harem there. If that is not enough, thrown in a young girl who calls him "senpai" and a cousin who wants to bed him from the get go and yup, the concept of crafting a dating sim is lost to a huge soup of generic harem elements.

    The girls are not as good as well
    For a show that mocks about game heroines and character templates, Saekano poorly uses whatever chance to innovate it had. Although the girls can be comical at points, their romantic behavior is so damn generic and unexplained they turn out to be as bland as the heroines the show makes fun of. Their cuteness gain points, but these days this department needs to truly shine to outdo the competition.

    It ends up as just another template anime
    This crowded girl cast, the harem inspiration, and the already generic character design overshadows what could've been a more interesting experience. You see, Welcome no NHK already tackled the crafting of videogames by teenagers and there we got a far smarter comedy questioning the behavior of NEETs and gamers and otakus in general. Saekano initially tries that, but the protagonist astonishingly surpasses his issues with his "3D girls" without any kind of hassle, becoming a women magnet whom every cute girl in 10 miles wants to bed.

    It does inspire for comedy though
    Althouh it fails its attempt to be a smart comedy, Saekano does have a power to make you smile and laugh from time to time. The overly used archetypes, some exagerated events, and the sole fact that a fifteen year old girl draws hentai material manage to achieve funny moments. Sadly, however, they are not as common as they should be.

    Ah, it doesn't end...
    Well, this is a norm these days, but is always a negative when a show doesn't offer any kind of conclusion neither tries to bring the last string of episodes to some sort of climax. It just ends. Sad...

    And A1-Pictures once again does a great job
    It's now a standard for A1-Pictures to deliver quality shows every season. Saekano is far from being as artistically charming as Your Lie in April or technically awesome as Aldnoah.ZERO, but it is a solid show nonetheless. The generic character templates are charming on their own, the scenery is beautiful enough and the animation is surprisingly good for the genre. I've already put in my mind that this studio is one to look out for, so if you had any doubt perhaps Saekano may make this clear.
Comments
I was expecting more, much more. Some people talked about Saekano as being a Shirobako with more comedy or as one of the funniest animes from its season, but what I got instead was a generic harem show with glimpses of comedy and a shallow attempt of approaching issues of otakus and the failings and pros of the industry of dating sims and similar games.

It still pleased me though, but more because I only wished for a time-killer than anything else. The super cute girls are there for those who like that and the loli factor is limited if you ignore that all girls look quite young, so it can be a visually interesting experience as well beyond the half-baked comedy parts. Perhaps if Saekano approached the otaku issues or the problems inherent to crafting a dating sim it would be a far better show. The worst of it, however, is how boring the protagonist grows to be, just a loudmouth otaku who initially claims to hate girls but instantly adapts and attracts every cute girl around him, much like what happened with Hyouka and hundred of other shows with "wannabe anti-social protagonists".
show review
by Zaku88 on 2015-06-01 04:02
Rating: 6.5
Approval: 97.2% (1 votes)
Saenai is about an otaku who claims to be interested only in 2D girls. However, he is suddenly inspired by a 3D girl at the top of a hill and instantly wants to turn that scene into the world's best gal-game. To do this, he must utilize the best possible talent, which happens to include many many female acquaintances including a tsundere who draws hentai works, a sharp-tongued script writer, and a carefree song writer. In essence, this is a light-hearted harem with a light focus on creating a game, something like Mangaka-san from a few seasons back.

The harem genre is pretty broad with a lot of works out there. This particular one focuses more on character interactions rather than action or moe heroines. The show scores high on playfulness but unfortunately not so much on relationship progress, it feels like there is an impenetrable wall preventing anyone from getting really close. If you're all right with what amounts to a lot of "foreplay" dancing around the central theme of making a game, then definitely give this a perusal. Oh, and keep in mind that episode 1 takes place after the other episodes; some things didn't make sense so maybe you might benefit by starting with #2.

Animation:
The animation, as with most other shows these days, is pleasant with only a light smattering of CGI effects. The series is drawn with a now-standard slightly under-saturated palette and most scenes take place in locations with relatively simple backgrounds, other shows like Grisaia still has better background drawings. That said the show did a pretty good job of drawing in unimportant bystanders, though they are sometimes blurred so the focus remains on the primary cast. The show uses colored outlines to emphasize certain scenes; whereas we saw magenta being used throughout No Game No Life, a more artistic approach is used here where various colors such as green or pink are used for the outlines. Though animated by a different company, the girls here have the same red eyelash highlight as seen in Grisaia. The cast is well drawn and easily distinguishable, you won't mistake a poster from this show with another show. As this is made by A-1 Pictures, you can expect a fair number of character cameos from other works as figurines or posters.

There isn't really any action in the show, and the ecchi portions are relatively light. Though generally well dispersed, you will find a particular emphasis on ecchi in the first episode (my sub provider aptly numbered the first episode #0); examples include obligatory onsen scenes, suggestive positions and upskirts.

Sound:
The show opens with a playful tune, which feels like something new for Luna Haruna; it's a fair melody but nothing particularly memorable. The ED is slightly slower but likewise playful with some interesting electronic notes. The BGM does its job but doesn't particularly stand out, it's pretty minimal as there are lots of scenes that are dialogue only.

Ai Kayano by herself stands as a pretty strong motivation to try out this show. Kiyono Yasuno has mostly done supportive role, so fits perfectly as heroine that behaves like a supporting character. Saori Oonishi likewise hasn't had a breakout role but plays a fine tsundere here. Yoshitsugu Matsuoka does a lot of screaming in SAO, and you'll hear some of that signature screaming here as well. In general, expect a pretty strong performance by the cast.

Story:
This is not an otaku's dream come true, as unlike most real otaku Tomoya knows the secret identity of some pretty famous creators. Once he finds a perfect model for a gal-game protagonist in Megumi, he works to recruit his fellow heroine creators to help him in his dream. Unfortunately, the road is a tough one as the other heroines all have their own real life work to do as well. What lengths will he go to convince them to join his cause?

This comedic harem starts a bit on the slow side as Tomoya takes a fair number of episodes to get rolling. Once he gets his act together, the show proceeds in a fairly linear fashion with him convincing the heroines to join one at a time, pretty much all the way to the very end. Though the show ends on a conclusive note, they don't actually get to finish the gal-game and there is already a sequel announced.

To be honest, the actual storyline isn't particularly interesting, as is the case with shows like Yappari; it's the character interactions that drive the show. The plot is well balanced between ecchi, character development, and plot progression; with emphasis placed on the middle element.

Character:
Speaking of character interactions, you'll get a lot of that here. Whether it is Tomoya trying to whip poor Megumi into 2D heroine shape or Utaha and Eriri battling it out, there is ample enjoyment. Tomoya is a pretty hardcore otaku who values his figurines like real pets. He serves as the compass for the group, essentially directing the group in a particular direction and meanwhile serving as the punching bag for all the heroines' whims. Megumi is a funny ninja, a person who is surprisingly outspoken and with little presence, despite her bishoujo appearance. It's not really what you'd look for as a star heroine but ironically Tomoya insists that it has to be her. Utaha feels similar to Senjougahara from Bakemonogatari, very skilled and smart but with bite to match (her Pocky-eating skills proves this). Eriri is more of your typical tsundere who constantly fails to get her point across and is constantly bullied by Utaha. The last heroine, Michiru, is the girl next door who doesn't really make an impact, mostly because she doesn't show up until the very end of the show; I assume her role may get a boost in the sequel.

I wouldn't call the characters dynamic as they all (Megumi aside) have some existing connections with Tomoya, and are just responding to his humorous pleas. There are a few additional supporting cast members that are used to good effect to further the plot.

Value:
To be honest, the show didn't leave that strong of an impression on me in comparison to other shows like Shigatsu. It's a fun watch for sure but just doesn't have the same enjoyment factor as competitors like Nisekoi. It's really more about the girls than the actual game that they're working to make. I give them credit for trying a new premise, and honestly making it further than Mangaka. If you're looking for a decent harem and are out of other options, give this a try.
